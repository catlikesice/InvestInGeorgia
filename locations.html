Locations

Dashbashi Canyon

Dashbashi Canyon captivates with its dramatic cliffs, a modern glass bridge spanning the gorge, and a unique diamond-shaped café perched above the valley. Nature lovers can enjoy hiking, birdwatching, and cycling while taking in the canyon’s stunning natural beauty.

Borjomi and Borjomi-Karajula National Park

Borjomi is famous worldwide for its mineral-rich springs, which have been attracting health seekers since the 19th century. The charming spa town is enveloped by the vast Borjomi-Kharagauli National Park, one of Europe’s largest protected landscapes. Visitors can wander through dense pine forests, explore diverse flora and fauna, and discover winding trails for hiking and wildlife spotting. Borjomi’s historic narrow-gauge railway connects adventurers to the nearby Bakuriani ski resort, creating a perfect mix of wellness, nature, and outdoor recreation. .

Chiatura

Chiatura stands out with its iconic Soviet-era cable cars that clatter across deep ravines, originally built to transport miners. From here, adventurers can reach the Katskhi Pillar, a striking limestone monolith crowned by a tiny medieval church, offering a glimpse into Georgia’s mysterious spiritual heritage.

Martvili Canyon

Martvili Canyon enchants with its clear turquoise waters flowing between moss-draped cliffs. Visitors can enjoy boat tours and nature walks in this lush environment, historically cherished as a bathing retreat for Georgian nobility. It’s a tranquil oasis for adventurers and photographers alike.

Kazbegi (Stepantsminda)

Kazbegi, or Stepantsminda, is famous for the Gergeti Trinity Church, dramatically perched on a hillside with Mt. Kazbek towering behind. The breathtaking alpine landscapes and well-marked trekking routes make it a destination beloved by hikers and nature photographers from around the world.

Ushguli (Svaneti)

Ushguli, one of Europe’s highest inhabited villages, lies deep within the Svaneti region. Renowned for its medieval Svan towers and culminating in majestic mountain views, this UNESCO World Heritage site offers travelers a rare glimpse into Georgia’s highland culture and pristine natural environment.

Tbilisi

Tbilisi, Georgia’s vibrant capital, is a fascinating blend of ancient history and modern culture, nestled in a valley along the winding Kura River and encircled by hills. The city’s Old Town is a picturesque mosaic of cobblestone streets, colorful balconied houses, and historic landmarks like the 4th-century Narikala Fortress, offering stunning views over the cityscape. The iconic Bridge of Peace, a modern glass and steel pedestrian bridge illuminated at night, elegantly spans the river linking Old Town with the contemporary Rike Park. Religious architecture such as the massive Holy Trinity Cathedral (Sameba), one of the largest in the world, and the centuries-old Sioni Cathedral highlight Tbilisi’s spiritual heritage. Visitors can also enjoy Mtatsminda Park atop Mtatsminda Hill, accessible via a funicular, featuring amusement rides and panoramic cafes. Tbilisi is a city of lively markets, diverse cuisine, and thriving arts, making it an essential gateway to Georgian culture.

Kutaisi

Kutaisi, one of Georgia’s oldest cities and the legislative capital, is steeped in history and surrounded by natural beauty. Located in the fertile Imereti region, it boasts landmarks like the Bagrati Cathedral, a UNESCO World Heritage site known for its impressive medieval architecture and sweeping views from its hilltop location. The nearby Gelati Monastery, another UNESCO site, is a masterpiece of Georgian Golden Age art and scholarship. Kutaisi serves as a convenient base for exploring the nearby Prometheus Cave with its dramatic stalactites and stalagmites, as well as the Sataplia Nature Reserve, famous for dinosaur footprints and pristine forest trails. The city’s lively central market, with fresh local produce and crafts, reflects the vibrant everyday life of western Georgia.

Batumi

Batumi, the dynamic capital of Georgia’s Black Sea coast, seamlessly blends modern urban energy with picturesque seaside charm. This cosmopolitan city is famous for its futuristic architecture, including the iconic Alphabet Tower and the strikingly curved Batumi Tower. The lively Batumi Boulevard stretches along the seafront, offering beautifully landscaped parks, fountains, sculpture gardens, and a pebble beach ideal for relaxation and water sports. The city’s vibrant nightlife scene, waterfront cafes, and casinos contribute to its reputation as a resort destination. Batumi also serves as a gateway to the Colchis Lowland’s unique subtropical nature, including nearby botanical gardens and protected wetlands. With a mix of Russian, Turkish, and Georgian influences, Batumi offers a culturally rich experience by the sea.
